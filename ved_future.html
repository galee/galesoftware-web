What is the future for car tax (VED) ?

In April 2017 we are going to see Changes to the current VED rates. Modern cars with their low emmissions are leaving a hole in the exchequers budget. The chances are you will be worse off from 2017 - see below for a few car comparisons of what you pay now for VED and what you will pay in for the same car next year.

What we're really interested in here is how we may approach vehicle taxation not in a couple of years but 10 years plus. Many would argue that the VED (vehicle excise duty) is an unjust approach to vehicle taxation. Whereby those who drive 1000 miles per year pay the same amount of fixed priced duty as someone who drives 50000 mlies per year. Even though, since 2001, VED costings have been largely based on the CO2 emissions of the vehicle, low mileage drivers are still relatively worse off. Through taxation and European legislation the cars we are driving are becoming cleaner and cleaner, leading to the current VED duty leaving a hole in the chancellors bank account.

To get around this loss of revenue from April 2017 there is a change to VED. There will be a change to the one off charge when the vehicle is bought. This one off purchase tax charge based on list price and CO2 emissions. Further years taxation will be £140 for all vehicles unless the list price of the vehicle is over 40,000 in which case it will be £450 for 5 years before dropping to £140 per year thereafter. Vehicles registered pre April 2017 will remain VED'wise as they are now.

One solution touted is to cancel the VED and all associated costs of the taxation and instead up the fuel duty. In fact such a scheme has been proposed, but no implemented, before in 2007 - the Lorry Road-User Charge. Currently fuel prices are 102 pence per litre (Jan 2016) of which duty makes up 58 pence. The total income to the exchequer of VED £6 billion and from fuel duty is 27 billion (2014). Therefore by upping the fuel duty by 20%, taking the fuel duty from 58p per litre to 68.6p per litre we could cover the 6 billion shortfall if VED was scrapped. This would mean a litre would currently cost nearly 115 pence per litre (inc. application of VAT). With the recent fall in fuel price this figure of 115p per litre seems OK, we were paying more than this in 2010. Sot he logical solution is to scrap VED and cover the offset by upping the fuel duty right?

Wrong, apart from aggravating high mileage lorry drivers and already Uber angry cabbies such an approach is not future proof. Electric vehicles are here and unlike the Sinclair C5 this time they are here to stay. The future is most likely electric with many manufacturers backing this type of vehicle over hydrogen due to the infrastructure already in place. Currently hydrogen fuel cell technology does, theoretically, have an advantage in the propulsion of large vehicles. But battery tech is undergoing major investment (The Tesla gigafactory in Nevada is alone receiving 5 billion dollars of investement). Unlike Hydrogen cars, which could have duty added to Hydrogen, Adding duty to household electricity is a difficult political proposition.

There was also an article on Volvo selecting 100 drivers in Sweden to test autonomous cars in a more general setting (on roads without bikes or pedestrians). I had a ride in a Tesla the other day. Not only did it do 250 miles per charge and 0-60 in 3.1 seconds it also came with the autopilot feature. This worked well for the straight road (Swansea sea front) we were on even though it was dark and there were several sets of traffic lights. This technology is moving apace with government and manufacturer buy-in. One day, probably sooner than we think,we'll all be transported from A to B in an efficient manner in our electric/hyrdrogen cars without even driving. Even the news last night said that children born now may perceive with amusement the quaint antics of us actually driving

In the future why own a car when an app can summon a driverless car, a driverless Uber if you like, to come and pick you up and transport you to your destination. Maybe you could share this transport with others going the same way. As most people's second most expensive asset a car spends an awful lot of its time doing nothing. 96% of the time actually.
